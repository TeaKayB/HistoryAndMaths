Bridging the Humanities and Mathematical Sciences in the Classroom

Welcome to The History and Mathematics in Education Network (HaMiEN). We are a collaborative community of primary and secondary school teachers, subject leaders, university researchers, museum and heritage educators, and curriculum innovators dedicated to exploring cross-curricular synthesis.

We believe in the potential for history and mathematics to deeply enrich educational experiences in both subjects. Exploring the history of mathematics reveals the human stories, motivations, and cultural breakthroughs behind abstract concepts. At the same time, recognising the mathematics woven into history allows students to see how developments in commerce, navigation, technology, and architecture were fundamentally built upon mathematical advancements. By bridging these fields, we bring students closer to a complete, connected understanding of human progress.

The History and Mathematics in Education Network (HaMiEN) connects teachers, museum educators, and researchers who are interested in exploring opportunities for cross-curricular enrichment between Mathematics and History.

For Classroom Teachers

Discover ready-to-use, dual-purpose ideas. Humanise abstract mathematics using historical narratives, and reveal the mathematical frameworks underpinning historical topics, geography data, and literary structures.

For Academic Researchers

Bridge the gap between pure historical scholarship and active secondary pedagogy. Connect directly with classroom practitioners to share peer-reviewed findings, test empirical models, and ground your research in modern educational practice.

For Museum Educators

Unlock the hidden STEM potential within your humanities collections. Collaborate with specialists to design interdisciplinary workshop models, create unique learning tracks, and turn material history into a tangible engine for mathematical discovery.

HaMiEN's Inception

The idea for the HaMiEN developed while Jason Yip and Tom Briggs began to share findings from a project conducted at the end of 2024. Many teachers that they spoke to agreed that the principles of using maths and history to each enrich the teaching of the other were enticing, but specialist teachers of each subject often brought up the same problem: they often didn't have much experience of the other subject, especially when it came to knowledge of curriculum content.

Jason

Jason is a PhD candidate and Academic Skills Adviser (Mathematics) at Middlesex University, specialising in the history of mathematics and mathematics education. A multilingual educator, he holds a BEd in Mathematics and Mathematics Education from the Chinese University of Hong Kong and an MA in Mathematics and Pedagogy from the Education University of Hong Kong. With experience across diverse educational stages, from elementary to tertiary, he is dedicated to enhancing mathematics education through historical and multicultural perspectives.
